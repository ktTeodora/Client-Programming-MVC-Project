Where did they come from?The Origin

One of the most adored and recognisable items that have been nurtured for centuries as a treasured element of Japanese art and culture, and are greatly admired by a vast number of foreigners, wagasa or Japanese umbrellas actually originate from China.

During the Heian Period (794-1185), a straw canopy hat called "tengai" first appeared and it gradually spread to Japan. These hats could not be opened or closed, and it wasn’t before the Muromachi Period two centuries later that the umbrellas were granted their recognisable shape. During this time, oil was painted on washi paper and it was finally possible to open and close an umbrella.
Today, wagasa are grouped in the following three types: Bangasa (sturdy umbrellas), Jyanome (slender umbrellas), and Japanese parasols (Higasa and Maigasa).

Where Are They Today?Art & Tradition

The art of creating a wagasa has been preserved for over a thousand years. The initial functionality has somehow fallen second to the value these umbrellas obtained in Japan and the weight they now hold as an integrated element of their tradition.

Today, we differentiate three different types of wagasa: Bangasa, Janome, and Maigasa. Bangasa is also referred to as a sturdy rainy umbrella and is the simplest out of the three in terms of design and patterning. Unlike this sturdy, rainy umbrella, Janome is a rather slender type where the design of the pattern on the wash paper can take a far more creative swing. For their design, colouring and patterns, they are oftentimes used not only for practical purpose, but also for decoration. Finally, Maigasa grants the artist complete freedom, as it allows for the colors of the washi paper and the stencil-dyed patterns of sun parasols to shine. In addition to Maigasa, there is a rather similar type of these sun parasols called Higasa and the two are often grouped together.

Who Are We?About Us

Simbelmynë is a wagasa atelier founded in 1864 and has been working diligently to satisfy the desires of our customers, as well as maintain the most delicate approach in preserving traditional values assigned to these stunning works of art.

All of our products are handmade and treated with utmost care. In production, we use all-natural bamboo and when creating the “temoto rokuro” (the wooden core) which server as the basis for the artwork in the making. The oil-paper cover that is intertwined with temoto rokuro is made out of wash paper materials, which is also refined and modelled by our artists. The process may take some time, but we guarantee quality and a product that honours the centuries long tradition and craftsmanship.
